If you're considering a 1031 exchange into a Delaware Statutory Trust, the most practical question you probably have is simple: **How much money will I actually receive each month?**

It's a fair question — and one that deserves a direct answer with real numbers, not vague projections or fine-print disclaimers that obscure more than they reveal.

This article breaks down exactly what a $500,000 DST investment can generate, how those returns compare to alternatives, what drives the variation in distributions, and what to watch for when evaluating DST offerings.

---

## The Baseline: DST Cash-on-Cash Returns

DST investments typically offer **cash-on-cash returns of 4%–6.5% annually**, paid as monthly or quarterly distributions.

Cash-on-cash return measures annual pre-tax cash income divided by the amount you invested. It does not include appreciation — only the ongoing income distributions from the property's operations.

Here's what $500,000 generates across that range:

| Annual Return | Monthly Income | Annual Income |
|---|---|---|
| 4.0% | $1,667 | $20,000 |
| 4.5% | $1,875 | $22,500 |
| 5.0% | $2,083 | $25,000 |
| 5.5% | $2,292 | $27,500 |
| 6.0% | $2,500 | $30,000 |
| 6.5% | $2,708 | $32,500 |

These are distributions from a single $500,000 DST investment. If you're exchanging a $1M or $2M property, multiply accordingly.

## How DST Returns Compare to Alternatives

To put these numbers in context, here's how DST distributions compare to other options for a retiring investor with $500,000 to deploy:

| Investment | Annual Income on $500K | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| DST | $20,000–$32,500 | Passive, tax-advantaged via depreciation |
| High-yield savings account | $22,500–$25,000 | Fully taxable, rate will decline |
| 10-year Treasury bonds | $22,000–$24,000 | Fully taxable, no inflation hedge |
| Dividend stocks (S&P 500 avg) | $6,500–$7,500 | 1.3–1.5% yield, significant volatility |
| REITs (publicly traded) | $15,000–$25,000 | Liquid but volatile, higher tax rate |
| Rental property (self-managed) | $25,000–$40,000 | Active management, maintenance, vacancies |
| Annuity (immediate, fixed) | $25,000–$30,000 | No appreciation, no heirs benefit, illiquid |

DSTs sit in a compelling middle ground: better income than bonds or dividend stocks, similar to high-yield savings but with real estate appreciation upside, and without the active management of a rental property.

One critical difference: **DST income benefits from depreciation pass-throughs that reduce your taxable income.** High-yield savings and bond income is fully taxable. This makes the after-tax comparison even more favorable for DSTs.

## The Depreciation Advantage: Keeping More of What You Earn

When you invest in a DST, you receive a proportional share of the property's depreciation deductions. Commercial real estate is depreciated over 39 years; residential over 27.5 years.

On a $500,000 investment in a property with standard leverage, you might receive $12,000–$18,000 in annual depreciation deductions. These deductions directly offset your income distributions — meaning a significant portion of your DST income may be **tax-sheltered**.

**Example:**
- Annual DST distributions: $27,500 (5.5% return)
- Depreciation deduction: $15,000
- Taxable income from DST: $12,500

Instead of paying taxes on the full $27,500, you only pay on $12,500. At a 22% federal tax rate, that saves you $3,300/year — an effective yield improvement of 0.66%.

This is one of the most underappreciated advantages of DST investing compared to other income-generating alternatives.

## Total Return: Income + Appreciation

Cash distributions are only part of the DST return story. When the DST sponsor eventually sells the underlying property (typically after a 5–10 year hold period), investors also receive their share of any appreciation.

Institutional-grade DST properties — Class A multifamily, industrial, medical office, net-lease retail — have historically appreciated at 2%–4% annually in normal market conditions.

**Hypothetical total return on $500K DST (7-year hold, 3% annual appreciation):**

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Annual distributions (5.5% × 7 years) | $192,500 |
| Appreciation (3% annual, compounded) | $112,000 |
| **Total return** | **$304,500** |
| **Return on $500K investment** | **61%** |

This is a hypothetical illustration, not a guarantee. DST returns are not guaranteed, and appreciation depends on market conditions at the time of sale. But it demonstrates why total return — not just current income — matters when evaluating DST offerings.

## What Drives Higher or Lower DST Returns?

Not all DSTs are created equal. Here's what determines whether you land closer to 4% or 6.5%:

### Property Type
- **Net-lease retail (Walgreens, Dollar General, etc.):** 5%–6.5%, long-term leases with rent escalations, minimal management
- **Multifamily (apartment complexes):** 4%–5.5%, steady demand, management-intensive
- **Industrial/warehouse:** 4.5%–6%, strong fundamentals driven by e-commerce
- **Medical office:** 4.5%–5.5%, sticky tenants, recession-resistant
- **Senior housing:** 5%–7%, higher return potential, operationally complex

### Leverage
DSTs with higher loan-to-value ratios tend to offer higher cash yields (because debt amplifies returns) but carry more risk. Conservative investors often prefer DSTs with moderate leverage (40%–55% LTV) for stability.

### Market and Location
Properties in high-growth Sun Belt markets (Texas, Florida, Arizona, Southeast) often project stronger appreciation. Properties in stable, supply-constrained markets offer more predictable income.

### Lease Structure
Triple-net (NNN) leases, where tenants pay taxes, insurance, and maintenance, provide the most predictable income because the DST's expenses are minimal. Gross leases, where the landlord covers expenses, have more variability.

### Sponsor Track Record
Sponsors with 20+ years of full-cycle experience tend to deliver more consistent results. A sponsor projecting 7% returns on a Class B multifamily in a secondary market is a much bigger risk than one projecting 5% on a Class A net-lease portfolio.

## What to Watch For: Red Flags in DST Projections

DST offering memoranda include projected returns, but these projections deserve scrutiny. Watch for:

**Return of capital masquerading as income.** Some DSTs fund distributions partly from investor capital rather than property operations. This inflates the apparent yield while actually reducing your investment. Look for "sources of distributions" language in the OM.

**Optimistic vacancy assumptions.** If a multifamily DST projects 96% occupancy in a market where comparable properties run at 92%, the income projection is inflated.

**Short-term fixed-rate debt.** DSTs with balloon payments on variable or short-term debt carry refinancing risk. If rates rise and the debt comes due, the sponsor may need to sell at an unfavorable time or restructure.

**Excessive fees.** Asset management fees, acquisition fees, and disposition fees all reduce your net return. Total fees across the hold period should be reasonable relative to the projected returns.

## Putting It All Together

For a retiring investor doing a 1031 exchange with $500,000 to reinvest, a DST offers:

- **Monthly income:** $1,667–$2,708 (4%–6.5% range)
- **Tax efficiency:** Depreciation deductions reduce taxable income significantly
- **No management:** Zero landlord responsibilities
- **Appreciation potential:** 2%–4% annually on institutional-grade properties
- **Estate planning:** Heirs receive stepped-up basis, eliminating deferred capital gains

The income won't make you rich — but it's designed to be reliable, passive, and tax-efficient, which is exactly what most retiring investors need.
