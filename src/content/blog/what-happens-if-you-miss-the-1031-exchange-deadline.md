You sold your rental property. Your Qualified Intermediary is holding the proceeds. And somehow — through bad luck, a deal falling through, or simply running out of time — you missed your deadline.

What happens now?

The short answer is painful: **a failed 1031 exchange triggers the full capital gains tax on your sale — immediately, with no further deferral options.** But the full picture is more nuanced, and in some cases, there are still moves you can make to soften the blow.

---

## The Two Ways an Exchange Fails

A 1031 exchange can fail in two distinct ways:

### 1. Missing the 45-Day Identification Deadline
If you don't submit a written identification of replacement property within 45 calendar days of your closing date, the exchange fails automatically. Your Qualified Intermediary must return your proceeds, and the full gain from your sale becomes taxable in the year of the sale.

### 2. Missing the 180-Day Closing Deadline
If you identified replacement property within 45 days but failed to close on it within 180 days, the exchange also fails — even if you were days away from closing. Any uninvested proceeds are returned to you and are taxable.

**Partial completion:** If you close on some but not all of your identified property, you can still complete a partial exchange. The portion you exchanged is tax-deferred; the cash that comes back to you (called "boot") is taxable.

## The Tax Bill: What You're Actually Looking At

Let's put real numbers on what a failed exchange costs. Assume:
- Sale price: $900,000
- Original purchase price (basis): $200,000
- Depreciation taken over the years: $100,000
- Net gain: $700,000

Here's the tax breakdown if the exchange fails:

| Tax | Rate | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Long-term capital gains (federal) | 20% | $140,000 |
| Net Investment Income Tax | 3.8% | $26,600 |
| Depreciation recapture | 25% | $25,000 |
| State income tax (avg ~5%) | 5% | $35,000 |
| **Total** | | **~$226,600** |

That's nearly a quarter million dollars — due when you file your taxes for the year of the sale. If you don't have the liquidity (because your proceeds were sitting in escrow waiting for a deal), this creates a serious cash flow problem.

## What Options Do You Have After a Failed Exchange?

### Option 1: Pay the Tax and Reinvest
The most straightforward option. Pay the capital gains tax and reinvest what remains into any investment you choose — stocks, bonds, a new rental, a DST as a non-1031 investment, a REIT, or simply an income-generating portfolio.

The upside: you're free. No lock-up periods, no illiquidity, no 1031 constraints on future decisions. The downside: you've permanently lost the deferred tax advantage, and your investable capital is significantly reduced.

### Option 2: Installment Sale (If You Haven't Closed Yet)
If your exchange is failing because a deal fell through and you're still within the exchange window, talk to your QI and tax advisor immediately. In some cases, you may be able to restructure the sale as an **installment sale** under IRC Section 453.

An installment sale spreads the recognition of capital gains across multiple years as you receive payments — which may keep you in a lower tax bracket each year and reduce the overall tax burden. This requires that the original sale be structured to allow installment treatment, so it's not always available.

### Option 3: Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) Investment
If your exchange has failed — or if you're within 180 days of the failure — you may be able to roll your gains into a **Qualified Opportunity Zone Fund** instead.

QOZ investments offer:
- Deferral of capital gains until 2026 (or when you sell the QOZ investment, whichever is earlier)
- Potential 10% reduction in recognized gain if held 5+ years
- **Complete exclusion of appreciation** on the QOZ investment itself if held 10+ years

QOZ investments are illiquid and complex, but for investors who've missed their 1031 window, they represent a legitimate second chance at tax deferral.

### Option 4: Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)
A Charitable Remainder Trust allows you to contribute appreciated property or cash proceeds to a trust, receive an income stream for life (or a term of years), avoid immediate capital gains tax, and leave the remainder to charity at death.

This is a sophisticated estate planning tool that doesn't eliminate tax — the trust pays it over time — but it converts a lump-sum tax bill into a lifetime income stream while providing a charitable deduction.

### Option 5: Nothing — Sometimes It's the Right Call
If you're in a low-income year, paying capital gains tax now may actually make sense. If your total income (including the gain) keeps you in the 0% or 15% long-term capital gains bracket, the tax hit is far smaller than it would be otherwise. Some investors deliberately choose not to complete a 1031 exchange in years when they have large losses or reduced income to offset.

## Situations Where the IRS Grants Extensions

The IRS is generally inflexible about 1031 exchange deadlines. However, there are narrow exceptions:

**Presidentially declared disasters:** Revenue Procedure 2018-58 provides automatic extensions for taxpayers affected by federally declared disasters. If your property is in a disaster area, you may automatically receive additional time.

**Service disruptions:** In extremely rare cases involving IRS service disruptions, relief has been granted. This is not something to count on.

**Qualified Intermediary bankruptcy:** If your QI becomes insolvent or files for bankruptcy while holding your proceeds, the IRS has granted extensions in some documented cases. This is why choosing a financially stable, bonded QI matters enormously.

Outside of these narrow exceptions, no extension exists. The deadlines are absolute.

## How to Avoid This Situation Entirely

The best way to deal with a failed exchange is to never have one. Here's how:

**Start before you sell.** The biggest mistake investors make is waiting until after closing to look for replacement property. By the time you close, you should already have 2–3 DST offerings in mind that you're prepared to invest in.

**Use DSTs as your backstop.** Even if you're hoping to exchange into another rental property or commercial building, always have a DST identified as your backup. DSTs can close in days, they don't fall through, and they're always available. Identifying a DST alongside your primary target gives you a safety net.

**Choose an experienced QI.** Your Qualified Intermediary is a critical partner. Use one with a long track record, proper bonding and insurance, and clear procedures. A weak QI can create administrative failures that jeopardize your exchange.

**Don't over-identify.** Identifying 10 properties you can't realistically close on doesn't help you. Identify 2–3 serious candidates — ones you've already begun due diligence on — and have a plan to close on at least one.

**Know your backup plan before you need it.** Talk to your tax advisor about QOZ options and installment sale treatment before closing, so you're not scrambling to learn about them after the exchange fails.

---

## A Note on Partial Failures

If you identified $800,000 in replacement property but only closed on $500,000 of it, you haven't completely failed. The $500,000 that closed is still tax-deferred. The $300,000 that was returned to you (as boot) is taxable.

In this case, working with a DST allows you to deploy the remaining cash quickly — often in the final days before the 180-day deadline — and reduce or eliminate the boot. Even late in the exchange period, DSTs can save investors from a significant tax hit.
